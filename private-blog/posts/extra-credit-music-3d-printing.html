Extra Credit: Music & Sound in Additive Manufacturing

by Sawyer Smith

12-Hole Playable Ocarina

The 12-Hole Playable Ocarina is a fully functional musical instrument created entirely through 3D printing. An ocarina is a traditional wind instrument that produces sound through precisely shaped resonance chambers, and this digital design demonstrates how additive manufacturing can recreate complex acoustic properties.

How 3D Printing is Used

This ocarina is printed as a single piece using FDM (Fused Deposition Modeling) technology. The creator designed the internal chamber geometry and finger hole placements to achieve accurate musical tuning across a full chromatic scale. The print requires no assembly—the acoustic chamber, finger holes, and mouthpiece are all formed during a single print job, showcasing the ability of 3D printing to create complex hollow geometries that would be difficult or impossible through traditional manufacturing.

Why It's Significant

This project is innovative because it democratizes musical instrument creation. Traditional ocarinas require skilled ceramic craftsmanship or expensive molds for mass production. With 3D printing, anyone with access to a printer can produce a fully tuned instrument for a few dollars in material cost. It also demonstrates that 3D printed objects can achieve the acoustic precision required for musical applications, opening possibilities for customized instruments, experimental designs, and accessible music education tools.

Idea: AE Ink — Small Desk Weather Display

by Sawyer Smith

For my project, I am designing AE Ink, a small desk weather display focused on showing the weather information I actually care about most at a glance. The main motivation is that most weather apps, including Apple Weather, make you click further into the app to see the "feels like" temperature, even though that is often the number that matters most for deciding how it will actually feel outside.

This is also inspired by a real user need beyond myself. My mom follows the weather closely and has mentioned similar frustrations, so I want to create something useful for both of us: a compact desk display that continuously shows the most important outdoor metric, especially real feel, while also including indoor environmental readings so the device becomes a simple everyday comfort monitor.

Who uses the product and why?

The main users are people who care about weather in a practical, everyday way, especially people who want useful information instantly without opening an app. This includes users like me, who want the real feel temperature visible all the time, and users like my mom, who regularly checks weather conditions throughout the day.

It would also appeal to people who like well-designed desk objects, smart home accessories, or ambient information displays. Instead of checking a phone repeatedly, the user can glance at the desk display and immediately understand both the outdoor conditions and how the indoor space feels.

What is significant about the design and why?

What makes this design significant is that it is not just another generic weather station. The product is centered around a very specific user frustration: important weather data is often hidden behind extra taps, and the interface priority is not always aligned with what users actually want to see first.

My version also combines function with a more elevated physical design. It will include a 3D printed enclosure and hardware to hold the screen and internal components, and I am considering a laser-cut wood front panel to make the final object feel warmer, more intentional, and more like a designed home object rather than an exposed electronics project. That wood-and-printed hybrid construction is one of the key ways my concept differs from standard plastic weather displays.

Does anyone really care, and if so, why?

Yes, I think people do care, because weather affects small decisions constantly: what to wear, whether to go outside, whether to open a window, whether the house feels too humid, and how comfortable the environment actually is. A lot of people do not want a full dashboard of weather graphs — they just want a few meaningful numbers presented clearly.

This idea matters because it turns weather data into something passive and visible instead of something buried in a phone app. It also solves a relatable annoyance with existing digital weather tools by prioritizing the metric that some users care about most: how it actually feels outside, not just the raw temperature.

Is this a marketable idea, and if so, how?

Yes, I think it could be marketable as a small, design-forward desk or home weather product. It could appeal to people who like practical desk accessories, minimal smart-home style objects, or giftable tech products for parents, home offices, and weather-conscious users.

The marketable angle is that it is not trying to compete with full-featured professional weather stations. Instead, it focuses on being a compact, attractive, user-centered display that highlights the most useful data first. If developed further, it could be sold as a finished device, a DIY kit, or even an open-source style project with downloadable 3D-printable files and customizable front panel options.

Related products / references

Here are a few existing weather display products that are somewhat related:

  • AcuRite Home Weather Station with Vertical Full-Color Display — shows indoor temperature and humidity, plus outdoor readings and derived values like dew point or heat index.
  • La Crosse Color LCD Weather Station — a desktop/tabletop weather station with indoor and outdoor temperature and humidity monitoring.
  • La Crosse Wireless Color Weather Station C85845V4 — includes indoor/outdoor temperature and humidity with a bright color display and comfort indicators.

These products are useful references, but my concept is different because I want the display to be more selective, intentional, and design-conscious, especially through the emphasis on real feel as the primary reading and the use of a 3D printed structure with a wood front panel.

Problem statement

Many weather tools provide too much information in the wrong order, forcing users to click into apps to see the values they care about most, such as the "feels like" temperature. AE Ink solves this by creating a small physical desk display that presents the most useful indoor and outdoor comfort information immediately, in a compact and more thoughtfully designed form.

The Next 30 Years of 3D Printing

by Sawyer Smith

I'm speculating that over the next 30 years, 3D printing will continue to become dramatically more user-friendly and will enter homes as a basic appliance rather than a niche hobby tool. Much like how printers, microwaves, or power tools became normal household items, I think 3D printers will follow the same path. The recent revolution of "unbox and perfect printing" led by companies like Bambu Lab and Prusa feels like just the beginning of that transition.

As the technology matures, printers will keep getting better, easier to use, faster, and cheaper. Calibration, tuning, and troubleshooting—once major barriers—are already being abstracted away. As that trend continues, owning a 3D printer will become a no-brainer for many people, simply another tool that enables creativity, repair, and small-scale manufacturing at home.

I also suspect that higher-end technologies like metal printing and SLS will follow a similar trajectory. While they are currently expensive and industrial, there's no fundamental reason they can't become more compact, safer, and accessible to advanced hobbyists over time, much like resin printing once did.

3D printing has been my favorite technology to watch develop. Comparing my first Prusa MK3 to my current Bambu H2D, the difference over a relatively short period of time is nothing short of incredible. If that pace continues, the next few decades could fundamentally change how people make things.
